Tab III
Narrative Analysis — Peter Brooks: Narrative Desire
Desire
The story operates two interlocking desires simultaneously. Thompson's desire is compound: to hold his job, to be seen as himself rather than as his name's association, and eventually to extract retribution — "a government-issued check, once a week for the next twenty-six weeks." But beneath these practical desires is a deeper and permanently frustrated one: to belong. He has lived two states from Boston his whole life and never been. He fabricates the memories because the desire to belong to the collective grief is stronger than the truth of his exclusion. The reader's desire is forensic: is he guilty of stealing the tip jar? Was the firing discriminatory? The story is engineered so that neither question can be resolved. This double irresolution is structural, not accidental — the story's argument depends on the impossibility of settling either charge through evidence alone.

There is also a ghostly third desire that haunts the text: the desire to be named correctly — to have one's name mean oneself, rather than someone else's atrocity. This desire is inarticulate in the story. Thompson never states it. But it is what the sixth-grade teacher's question ("Have you ever thought about changing your name to Tommy?") and his mother's answer ("You should be proud") are both, inadequately, responding to.
Deferral
Deferral in this story operates through three distinct registers. First, evidential deferral: the tip jar theft is never solved. The perpetrator is withheld permanently. This means Thompson's guilt or innocence cannot be established, and the reader — like the manager, like the EEOC — must decide on the basis of inference and suspicion rather than fact. Second, causal deferral: the story implies but never confirms that Thompson was fired because of his name. "These events were, of course, unrelated" introduces precisely the suspicion it denies. The manager's letter denies everything, admits nothing — and the denial cannot be disproved. Third, structural deferral: the revelation that the "owner" was only the manager is withheld until the final pages. This deferral is decisive. The alternate narrative Thompson constructs — in which his firing was personal, purposeful, and discriminatory — collapses not because it is disproved, but because its antagonist turns out to be an average middle manager navigating the same institutional precarity from the other side of the counter. What is withheld until the end is not the verdict but the frame of the trial.
The Middle
Brooks argues that the middle is the site of desire's greatest intensity — the narrative's resource of deferral, repetition, and elaboration. Sayrafiezadeh fills his middle with bureaucratic process: the unemployment office, the EEOC, the Starbucks. Each institution repeats the structure of the coffee shop (sit across a table, answer questions, perform eligibility) while transforming Thompson's relationship to power. The middle is also where the story performs its sharpest satirical observation: justice is not denied so much as postponed indefinitely. "The commission was backlogged. Never mind that the case counsellor appeared to have no other clients except for me." The middle's comedy is the comedy of dilation — each institutional visit extends the narrative and defers resolution by precisely the same mechanism: process.

The Starbucks interlude is the middle's most formally precise episode. The interview is described in language that mirrors the coffee shop interview word for word. This is the story's structural joke: Thompson has escaped one site of precarity by entering an identical one with better benefits. "If it weren't for the green apron and the twentysomething assistant manager and the fact that tip jars were not permitted, I could have been right back where I had been in April." The middle does not progress; it replicates.
The Ending
Brooks describes endings as the point at which accumulated narrative desire is satisfied, displaced, or exposed as impossible. This story performs all three at once, and the tonal result is ironic deflation rather than resolution. Thompson wins — formally. He receives $800 and a compulsory apology, both delivered by a man who turns out to have had far less power than Thompson attributed to him. The owner looks "uncomfortable and out of place, his power diminished, an average guy in a bad suit." Thompson feels "a sharp sense of remorse for having caused this ordeal." The alternate narrative of conspiracy and retribution collapses at the moment of its ostensible victory.

The final sentence — "Starbucks was coming. Starbucks was going to swallow everything" — refuses to let the resolution stand as meaningful. Thompson has achieved his petty justice; he works for the corporation that will erase the coffee shop; the system grinds on indifferently. Brooks' notion of the ending as the point at which "desire is shown to be futile" is given here its most sardonic American form. Justice, $800 at a time, doesn't change the system. It is the system.
Narrative Energy
The story's forward energy — its libidinal economy, in Brooks' terms — derives from an unusual source: not suspense about outcomes, but uncertainty about the narrator himself. Thompson is neither reliable nor unreliable in the conventional sense; he is interpretively motivated. He knows his alternate narrative might not be true. He includes it anyway. He fabricates Boston memories and nearly believes them. He invokes "rights" without knowing what they are. He shifts "instinctively" into legalese. The reader is pulled forward not by wanting to know what happens next but by trying to decide whether this man is a victim of discrimination or of coincidence, and whether those are even distinguishable categories when the evidence is a name.

The story also generates energy through structural repetition — the interview scenes, the "I said yes to everything" formula, the recurring "I still hadn't heard back from any local retail businesses." Each repetition is a small deferral engine, sustaining the narrative by refusing to resolve. The green spiral notebook marked "Management" appears in the job interview, the bombing scene, the basement firing, and the settlement meeting — carrying the energy of institutional power across every encounter. The notebook is the story's most efficient symbol: it contains the records of a man who thought he was the owner and was only ever the manager.
Thematic Threads
ThemeDevelopment in the Story
The name as accusationThompson shares his first name with Tamerlan Tsarnaev by coincidence of ancestry. The story asks whether coincidence of naming can constitute cause for discrimination — and refuses to answer. The name becomes a structuring absence: the bombing is not mentioned to the manager, the name's resonance is never invoked explicitly, and the EEOC settlement carefully avoids confirming what everyone in the room understands.
Narrative construction of grievanceThompson explicitly identifies the moment his "alternate narrative" forms — "at first dreamlike and then self-evident." He acknowledges it may not be true; he knows only that it is possible. The story tracks how a plausible narrative of wrongdoing is assembled from random events, and what happens when that narrative meets the bureaucracy designed to adjudicate it. The final revelation is that the antagonist of the alternate narrative was himself navigating institutional precarity from the other side.
Fabrication and belongingThompson invents Boston memories he has never lived — twice — and nearly believes them. The fabrication is not mere dishonesty but a symptom of exclusion: he cannot share the collective grief because he has never been to Boston despite living two states away his whole life. The invented memories are his attempt to claim membership in a community that his name has simultaneously made him suspect in.
Institutional machinery as comedyEach institution Thompson enters (coffee shop, unemployment office, EEOC, Starbucks) replicates the same structure: sit across a table, perform eligibility, receive a verdict. The comedy is bureaucratic — justice is not denied but indefinitely processed. The EEOC counselor's repeated "I know this isn't easy" is the story's funniest line precisely because it is so adequately sincere and so inadequately comforting.
Surveillance and American innocenceThe American flag partially covers the coffee shop window, tinting everything red, white, and blue. The video camera is installed after the bombing "to make everyone feel safe." These patriotic gestures frame Thompson's workspace with a surveillance apparatus that will be used against him. The flag-filtered light is the story's most compressed image: American belonging offered and withheld simultaneously.
The mirror structure of interviewsThe coffee shop interview and the Starbucks interview are described in nearly identical language — same table corner, same milk and sugar, same checklist questions. The firing and the settlement meeting also mirror each other: both occur across a table, both feature the green notebook. The story is built on structural repetitions that expose the protagonist's inability to escape the conditions that constrain him, however much the surface details change.
Prose Style
Sayrafiezadeh writes in a dry, observational first-person that belongs to a tradition of American social comedy running from Ring Lardner through early Carver: declarative sentences, understated irony, deadpan accumulation of humiliating detail. His narrator notices everything and interprets selectively — a combination that produces a distinctive effect of simultaneously reliable reporting and unreliable inference. The sentences are short to medium, precise, and occasionally punctuated by a longer elaboration that reveals the narrator's class anxiety or historical self-consciousness ("he had taken that fateful trip to Boston to visit Faneuil Hall Marketplace and had come back home inspired").

Repetition is the story's key formal device. "I said yes to everything. I said no to nothing" appears twice. "I still hadn't heard back from any of the local retail businesses" is a recurring refrain. The green spiral notebook recurs like a talisman. "Next guest, next guest" echoes through the coffee shop scenes. These repetitions are not lyrical (as in Li) but structural — they mark the machinery of precarity clicking through its cycles. The story's most characteristic effect is the long parenthetical that opens a subordinate clause and then elaborates far beyond what the main sentence requires, mimicking Thompson's tendency to contextualize and qualify rather than confront: "named after my great-grandfather on my mother's side (or was it my father's?), whom I had never met, and who had immigrated to the United States from what was then Turkestan and is now Uzbekistan, and which, either way, I would be hard pressed to locate on a map."

Discussion Questions
Close Reading
The echo is almost certainly deliberate. Kafka's novel opens: "Someone must have been telling lies about Josef K., for without having done anything wrong he was arrested one morning." Sayrafiezadeh's sentence maps almost exactly onto this template — an accusation without stated grounds, a proceeding that advances without premises ("No premise needed"), a protagonist who invokes rights without knowing what they are. But where Kafka's bureaucracy is existential and surreal, Sayrafiezadeh's is material and mundane: a Department of Labor waiting room with fluorescent lights and number 93. The Kafkaesque machinery in this story does not distort reality — it reproduces American institutional reality with documentary precision. The comedy and the horror occupy the same sentence because the sentence is accurate. This is a useful distinction for undergraduate discussion: where Kafka literalises the feeling of accusation into nightmare, Sayrafiezadeh demonstrates that the nightmare is already the literal.
The repetition marks Thompson's consistent posture toward institutional power: total compliance in exchange for access. At the interview, it signals desperation to pass a checklist of employability — self-starter, problem-solver, team player. At the settlement, it signals deflation: he accepts $800 without negotiation, just as he accepted any working condition at the interview. The repetition also exposes the story's structural irony: the person who said yes to everything at the interview has spent months pursuing retribution for what happened at that job. The compliance that secured the job is the same compliance that ends the grievance. He has learned nothing — or perhaps learned exactly this: that saying yes to everything is the only available mode of participation in institutional life, and that it doesn't protect you.
Desire & Character
This is the story's sharpest psychological observation. Thompson identifies the moment of the alternate narrative's formation precisely: "at first dreamlike and then self-evident." He acknowledges the narrative's uncertainty even as he commits to it. This is a precise diagnosis of how grievance operates: we do not require certainty, only plausibility — and plausibility, in Thompson's case, is genuinely available. The story then tests the narrative through the EEOC process: the counselor agrees with it. But the final revelation (the "owner" was only a manager) doesn't refute the alternate narrative so much as dissolve the frame in which it was meaningful. The reader has been constructing their own alternate narrative alongside Thompson's — deciding whether the firing was discriminatory, whether the accusation was racially/culturally motivated. The story implicates the reader in the same evidential problem it assigns to its protagonist. Our sympathy is neither fully validated nor fully withdrawn.
The fabrication is the story's thematic heart. Thompson cannot share the group's collective grief for Boston because he has never been there, despite living two states away his whole life. His exclusion from the memorial is a structural mirror of the exclusion enacted by his name: in both cases, he is adjacent to an identity he cannot claim. He patches together others' stories "to make it my own" — and the fabrication works too well. This is precisely what his name has done to him from the other direction: Tamerlan Tsarnaev's identity has been grafted onto Thompson's, unbidden and inescapable. He invents Boston memories for the same reason he will later construct an alternate narrative of his firing — because the real events leave him with no coherent, acceptable self to present. The fabrication is not dishonesty so much as a symptom of the condition the story is diagnosing: the pressure on a person whose name makes the true account of themselves implausible.
Deferral & Structure
This is the story's most consequential structural choice, and it is precise rather than evasive. If we learned who stole the tip jar, the story would bifurcate into either a vindication narrative (Thompson is innocent, the firing was discriminatory) or a comic-irony narrative (Thompson is guilty, the firing was justified). Either resolution would allow the reader to settle the question of Thompson's character and move on. By withholding the answer permanently, the story refuses that consolation. We are in the same position as the manager, the EEOC, and Thompson himself: we must decide on the basis of coincidence, inference, and plausibility — which is exactly the epistemological condition the story is about. The unresolved whodunnit is not a plot hole; it is the plot. The story argues that guilt and innocence cannot be established when the charge is contaminated by association, and it enacts this argument formally by never supplying the evidence that would cut through the contamination.
Each institution raises and then defeats Thompson's expectations in a distinct register. The coffee shop raises the expectation of belonging (he says yes to everything; he wants to be a team player) and defeats it with expulsion. The Department of Labor raises the expectation of procedural fairness and defeats it with a technicality ("you didn't work there long enough to qualify") — justice foreclosed by its own mechanisms. The EEOC raises the highest expectation — Thompson imagines himself "ascending from state to federal, from wronged to revenge" — and delivers the most technically satisfying outcome ($800, an apology) paired with the most destabilizing revelation (the "owner" was only the manager). The institutions do not escalate in justice so much as in irony. The parallelism suggests that no individual case can exceed the limits of the system processing it: every institution produces the same table, the same corner, the same milk and sugar.
Endings & Meaning
This is the story's most formally sophisticated move. The alternate narrative Thompson has spent months building casts the "owner" as an antagonist with power, property, and purposeful aversion. The settlement scene produces a third narrative: the "owner" was not an owner at all but a middle manager who had to take time off work to attend a federal mediation. He is "not the outsized entrepreneur that I had always thought him to be, but, rather, one of those fortunate employees who had worked long enough at the coffee shop to qualify to enroll in college courses." The irony is devastating: Thompson's supposed oppressor was in almost the same structural position — a worker, not a proprietor, navigating institutional power from below. The alternate narrative is not disproved; it is dissolved. And Thompson's remorse ("I felt a sharp sense of remorse for having caused this ordeal") is genuine, but it cannot undo the triplicate forms. The story suggests that the pursuit of individual justice against systemic discrimination may be most likely to harm the person just above you in the same hierarchy — not the actual owner of anything.
The closing is among the most precisely ironic endings in recent American short fiction. Thompson has won: $800, an apology, a promotion at Starbucks. The compulsory apology ("as if we were children in the schoolyard") deflates the settlement to the level of a playground dispute. Then the final two sentences negate the individual victory entirely — not just the coffee shop, not just the manager's dignity, but everything will be consumed by the corporation Thompson now works for. He is both the beneficiary of the system and its agent. He will be the Starbucks that swallows the next coffee shop, the next Melissa, the next person who doesn't work long enough to qualify for anything. The manager's answer to Thompson's demand for justification — "No premise needed" — turns out to be the system's answer too, writ large. Starbucks needs no premise. Individual justice is achieved; structural justice is foreclosed. The question of whether Thompson was discriminated against is rendered simultaneously answered (he got his check) and irrelevant (it changes nothing about the conditions that produced the discrimination).

Biography

Saïd Sayrafiezadeh was born in Hamburg, West Germany in 1968 to an Iranian father, Mahmoud Sayrafiezadeh — a committed member of the Socialist Workers Party — and an American Jewish mother. His parents separated early, and he grew up primarily with his mother in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, a childhood shaped by economic precarity, political idealism, and the peculiar loneliness of the American left. His father's revolutionary commitments meant years of near-absence.

His memoir When Skateboards Will Be Free (2009) documents this childhood with sharp, unsentimental honesty. His fiction — collected in Brief Encounters with the Enemy (2013) and American Estrangement (2019) — ranges across class anxiety, military service, and the experience of feeling structurally excluded from the American identity one nominally inhabits. His work is persistently concerned with men who are peripheral to the systems that determine their lives.

He has been a regular contributor to The New Yorker for over a decade and has taught creative writing at MFA programs in New York. "Process of Elimination" — published in The New Yorker's May 4, 2026 issue — represents his characteristic mode: dry, precise social realism in which a comedy of institutional failure masks a genuine diagnostic of American belonging and its preconditions.
