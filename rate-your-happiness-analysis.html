Plot Analysis

Order

The sjuzhet is radically non-linear, opening in medias res as Louise faints aboard a flight to San Francisco. From this present-tense crisis, Lacey orchestrates a sustained cascade of analepses — Louise's career history, her anger's evolution mirroring Harry's, the Oaxaca cockroach, the decade-ago smartphone refusal — before returning to the present. There are no true prolepses; the story's "future" gestures are Louise's ironic interior projections (Diana in a veil), which Genette would classify as pseudo-prolepses or internal anticipation. The fabula covers roughly fifteen to twenty years of Louise's life compressed into what amounts to about four days of diegetic time in San Francisco.

Duration

Lacey systematically violates equivalence between sjuzhet and fabula duration. Scenes of high emotional charge (the galley floor exchange between Louise and Bruce; the cab ride and voice-note playback) are rendered at near-isochrony — one to two pages per real-time minute. Career history spanning fifteen years occupies one paragraph (summary). The ants-in-Oaxaca memory, though only a 27-second video, receives two paragraphs of narration, illustrating Lacey's preference for pause — the kind Genette identifies when the narrator slows or stops plot to expand description or reflection — at symbolically freighted moments. Sleep is an ellipsis; morning is cut to.

Frequency

The narrative deploys a mixture of singulative and iterative modes. The in-flight crisis, each conversation with Bruce, the cab ride, the Headlands hike, and the cosmetics store are singulative (narrated once, occurred once). Diana's communication habits are described iteratively — "she often communicated in paragraph-long texts" — as is Louise's pattern of almost-breaking-up-but-getting-sucked-in-deeper. Harry's anger and Louise's own anger are summarised as long-running iterative arcs. The Rate Your Happiness influencer's videos appear to Louise through Lynn's satellite phone as a rapid montage of the iterative, suggesting the social compulsion to quantify feeling is itself a repeated, mechanical act.

Voice & Focalization

The narrative is heterodiegetic (third-person) but tightly restricted to Louise's internal perspective — a classic case of Genette's internal focalization with a single, stable focaliser. The narrator maintains a dry, ironising distance from Louise without ever fully vacating her consciousness: the sentence "She was not an adorable and delicate child who deserved the care of helpful grownups but, rather, a 1099-receiving adult" carries both Louise's self-awareness and the narrator's judgement simultaneously. Shifts in focalization occur when Louise imagines others' interior states (Diana joining a support group, Bruce's "little disappointment" that the emergency was not an aneurysm) — these are what Genette might call focalisation transgressions, brief and productive.

Prose Style Note

Lacey's sentences accumulate subordinate clauses in a way that enacts Louise's overloaded, parenthetical consciousness — the famous "comma-spliced avalanche" that reviewers noted in Nobody Is Ever Missing appears here in controlled form, particularly in the opening paragraph. Free indirect discourse slides between Louise's self-awareness and ironic self-pity. The story's final image — a tube of eye serum promising internal illumination, pocketed as shoplifting — is Lacey's characteristic mode: the bathetic epiphany, refusing catharsis while offering something stranger and more durable.

Close Reading

The opening move is a studied deflation of the conventional near-death epiphany: where one would expect revelation or love, Louise produces shame and clutter. The "humiliating artifacts" — the skin-rejuvenation brochure, the worn romance novels, the decaying takeout — are self-image objects, things she hides from others. The closing scene rhymes precisely: Louise, standing in a cosmetics store surrounded by mirrors, pockets a tube of eye serum "promising to illuminate her from within." The story closes its loop not with transformation but with another object of aspiration, equally pathetic, equally human. Lacey withholds irony just enough to make the gesture also tender — the serum is still a form of wanting.

Bruce's pain-scale question is lifted from clinical triage protocol, but Louise's refusal — "No" — is the story's thesis in miniature. The influencer's exercise, we learn, is rigged: anyone who rates below eight gets drilled until they revise upward. Both scenes expose quantification as coercive rather than diagnostic. Louise's interiority is precisely what resists the scale; the narrative form of the story — dense, accumulative, unresolved — performs the same resistance. Lacey frames happiness not as a score but as a texture, present in flashes ("sometimes is good," Joan says) and impossible to totalise. The influencer is a mise en abyme of the story's own question, held at ironic arm's length.

Character

Bruce is Louise's mirror in the key of openness: he volunteers his emotions (tearing up over his father, crying in the crystal store), seeks connection, and is moved by coincidence. Louise is closed — she registers gratitude inwardly while deflecting it outwardly, she edits what she tells Joan and Lynn, she cannot ask Bruce directly whether he and Harry were romantically involved. The open-house scene crystallises their difference: both consider, in silence, an apartment in a city they openly dislike. When Diana calls and the lights go out, Louise hangs up — an instinct toward flight. Bruce, by contrast, continues making demands for contact even as he says goodbye. His care for Louise — literal, nursing-care — is the only unsolicited warmth she receives; her response is ambivalence dressed as fondness.

The Eldest Daughter is Louise at her most unmediated: rigorous, contemptuous of compromise, suspicious of techno-optimism, socially abrasive. Louise's adult self has learned to soften and deflect, but the child has not. When Louise catches her own voice echoing the girl's acerbity ("Why not?" / "Compared to what?"), Lacey suggests that Louise's alienation is not a product of age or circumstance but a persistent interior disposition. The "little-sister feeling" Louise experiences around the Eldest Daughter inverts the power dynamic: the child possesses the authority of consistency that Louise lacks. The aunt-niece relationship — an elective kinship, not blood — also rhymes with the story's preoccupation with inherited and chosen attachments.

Theme

The voice-note is a singular formal rupture: Harry speaks in his own voice, yet Louise has controlled when we hear it (saved, never replied to, retrieved in the cab). Lacey lets Harry's argument — that Louise "wants to suffer," that the "culture" has manufactured her unhappiness — stand without explicit refutation. The effect is deeply uncomfortable precisely because the reader has already spent pages inside Louise's consciousness and knows its texture. Harry's gaslighting depends on an abstract "culture" as agent; Louise's grief is concrete and specific. The two-year delay in playing it back (and the cab driver's tactful silence) creates a strange doubled temporality: Harry's monologue arrives as anachrony within the story's present, insisting that the past was different than Louise remembers it. Memory, Lacey implies, is not a neutral record but a contested site.

Louise's narrated hatred of San Francisco — its joggers, its startup pitches, its ergonomic men, its driverless cars — is itself a form of attention, which is a form of belonging. She is most fully present in the city when she is cataloguing what she resents about it. The driverless cars function as the story's dominant symbol of technological optimism unmoored from human purpose: they stall in the blackout, their headlights on, "in confusion, in paralysis, lacking any traffic lights to tell them what to do." Louise's closing meditation — "how natural it is to fail, to fail to decide, to remain in meaningless motion, like all those ants with their cockroach corpse" — explicitly identifies the cars with the Oaxaca image and with herself. The open house she visits with Bruce — an apartment in a city she disdains — is the story's comedic but earnest image of the desire for somewhere to land.

Form and Narrative Structure

In realist fiction, coincidence is usually a structural embarrassment — evidence of the author's hand. Lacey foregrounds it with such insistence ("What are the chances!") that it becomes ironic commentary on plot mechanics. The repeated phrase, spoken by characters who are delighted by the improbable, draws attention to the ease with which humans narrativise chance as meaning. For Louise, coincidence is more threatening than comforting: each reappearance of Bruce tightens a web of obligation around Harry. The old woman who caught Louise on the plane reappears on the hike and, in her dementia, says "Your father —" — as if coincidence and the paternal wound are the same force. Lacey suggests that stories (and the Rate Your Happiness influencer's project) are machines for converting randomness into legible feeling.

Genette distinguishes between narrative closure (the text ends) and story resolution (the fabula reaches stability). Lacey provides the former while deliberately withholding the latter. Louise leaves with "contradictory desires" held simultaneously — she has not called Harry back, has not ended things with Diana, has not decided anything about Bruce. "Total clarity" is the paradox: she is clear about her ambivalence, not clear about its resolution. The shoplifted serum — promising illumination from within — is a joke on the therapeutic-commodity complex, but it is also Louise's first unilateral, decisive act in the story. It is small, illicit, and self-directed. Lacey's open endings ask readers to resist the desire for narrative payoff, to sit with irreducible complexity in the way the pain scale cannot contain — the same refusal Louise voices in "No."

Short Biography

Catherine Lacey grew up in Tupelo, Mississippi — the same town where a young Elvis Presley bought his first guitar at her grandfather's hardware store — in a close-knit, church-intensive Southern family. She left for boarding school at fourteen, later studying at Loyola University New Orleans and earning an MFA from Columbia University in 2010.

Her debut novel Nobody Is Ever Missing (2014) announced a distinctive voice — lyrical, restless, self-interrogating — that Dwight Garner called "dreamy and fierce at the same time." Subsequent novels have moved from satirical (The Answers) to fable-like (Pew) to formally audacious (Biography of X, her breakout bestseller). Her 2025 hybrid work The Möbius Book defies genre classification entirely.

She has held fellowships at the Guggenheim Foundation and the NYPL's Cullman Center, taught at Columbia, the University of Montana, and the University of Mississippi, and now lives in Mexico City with her husband, the novelist Daniel Saldaña París. Her short fiction appears regularly in The New Yorker, Harper's, and The New York Review of Books.
