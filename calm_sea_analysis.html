Narrative Analysis — Peter Brooks: Narrative Desire
Desire
The story opens two desires simultaneously. Lilian's desire is to guarantee her children a "calm sea and prosperous voyage" — the Goethe/Mendelssohn dream she knows is impossible. This desire is explicitly elegiac: both sons are already dead before the first paragraph ends. The reader's desire is different: we want to understand how the boys died, whether the children on this trip are safe, and whether Lilian's grief can be metabolised into meaning. The story activates desire by disclosing death early but withholding cause, circumstance, and emotional resolution entirely.

Hazel carries a secondary desire: the desire of a child who cannot be "molded into an acceptable shape" to be seen accurately — not saved, not optimised, but genuinely witnessed. Lilian is the only adult who attempts this.
Deferral
Deferral operates on three levels. First, structural deferral: the deaths of Oscar and Jude are stated but never dramatised. No death scene appears. The narrative distributes grief across every moment of the field trip. Second, syntactic deferral: the em-dash after "Evan —" enacts the unfinishable nature of grief for the living. Third, tonal deferral: Lilian consistently reaches for displaced objects — a Jin dynasty poem, a melting snowman, cicadas — rather than speaking directly of loss. The story is itself an act of deferral, finding talismans against the grief it cannot resolve.
The Middle
The field trip is the story's sustained middle — five days of pastoral that are also a kind of temporal sanctuary. Brooks argues that the middle is the site of desire's greatest intensity, where the narrative finds reasons not to end. Li achieves this through iterative narration: "There was the hike… there was the coastal walk…" Each "there was" is an incantation, a repeated gesture of preservation. The bonfire scene is the middle's apex: children filing into the dark with a lamp, an image of concentrated beauty that brings tears to Lilian's eyes — not for what is lost, but for what she knows cannot last.
The Ending
Brooks calls the ending the point at which accumulated desire is satisfied, displaced, or exposed as impossible. This story refuses all three in their conventional forms. Hazel is found — the sjuzhet's crisis resolves — but the finding does not save her: her eyes hold "the same despair" Lilian will later recognise in Oscar's. The final question — "How can you mandate hope and optimism when no one can save us from our outliers' lot?" — is addressed to Lilian by Hazel, by Oscar, and by Jude simultaneously. The story ends not with resolution but with a question that cannot be answered. Its structure enacts its argument.
Narrative Energy
The story's forward energy is not suspense — we know the outcome before the plot begins. Its energy is memorial: the drive to recover and preserve particular moments against the obliterating force of loss. Every description of the children is animated by the knowledge that this is the last time they will be this young, this alive, this unaware of their futures. The narrative tension is between the desire to stay in the pastoral middle and the narrator's inability to unknow what happens next. This is Brooks' "desire of the text" at its most refined: the story moves forward because it must, but mourns every step.
Thematic Threads
ThemeDevelopment in the Story
Outlier fatesThe story asks whether outlier children (Hazel, Evan, Jude) meet outlier fates — and answers obliquely: they do. The school's failure with Ginny/Jude, and the principal's language of "male aggression," show the institution's incapacity to protect the outlier.
The wall between present and futureRebecca West's "wall" becomes a structuring image. Parents cannot stop trying to see through it; the story's temporal oscillations enact this compulsion. The cicada digression and Denmark scene both dramatise Lilian looking through a wall she cannot pull down.
Mandatory hope / obligatory optimismThe story is hostile to demanded consolation. Jeremy's speech to Hazel — "we will defer to you… we won't force you" — is the ethical alternative: accompaniment without coercion.
Children as beautiful and vulnerableThe children are "sculptures from ancient Rome or paintings from the sixteenth century." Beauty is inseparable from fragility. The water-insect metaphor positions childhood as a brief structural order before the inevitable scattering.
Grief's displaced objectsLilian finds proxy objects — the snowman, the cicadas, the swallows, the Bakkhai translation — rather than speaking directly of loss. These are the story's talismans: inadequate, precious, the only form of expression available to a grief too large for direct statement.
Calm sea as impossible dreamGoethe's poem and Mendelssohn's overture haunt the title. "Calm sea and prosperous voyage" is what every parent desires and what the story systematically denies. The Pacific "glistened colorlessly" — beautiful and indifferent.
Prose Style
Li writes in a long, composed periodic sentence that accumulates subordinate clauses before landing — or refusing to land — on a main verb. The syntax performs Lilian's consciousness: circling, qualifying, reaching for precision even when the subject resists it. The story uses iterative summary to compress days into museum-case arrangements, each memory preserved but slightly distanced. Dialogue is economical and character-revealing: Hazel's questions adversarial, Jude's answers exact, Jeremy's speech to the hiding girl quietly masterful. The story's most controlled effect is tonal: sustained elegiac calm, never breaking into open grief, even when announcing deaths flatly in subordinate clauses. This restraint formally embodies Lilian's discipline — and the story's critique of mandatory optimism.

Discussion Questions
Close Reading
The retrospective frame converts the field trip from a story with an open future into an elegy with a fixed past. Every detail the narrator notices — Jude's chubby face, Evan's way of speaking for his friend, the lamplight vanishing and returning in the dark — is charged with a grief that the reader shares before any of it is explained. This is dramatic irony at the structural level: we know both sons die; the children do not know they are being mourned. The frame also signals the story's central argument: that parents cannot stop trying to see through the wall between present and future, even when knowing the future destroys the present's innocence.
The em-dash marks the point at which Lilian cannot complete the sentence about Evan — not because she doesn't know his fate, but because she has chosen not to know it: "I did not know what he would be like as a young man, only that his faring would never be easy." The grammar enacts the ethical refusal: Lilian will not project Evan's future the way she (she believes) erroneously projected the children's futures during the trip. The dash is also the story's most condensed image of surviving grief: the sentence continues for the living even when it cannot be finished for the dead.
Desire & Character
Lilian sees Ophelia in Hazel — a connection she thinks but does not say aloud, recognising it as "an odd comment." This withheld recognition is significant: Lilian perceives the danger in Hazel's fierce self-sufficiency but cannot intervene meaningfully. What Lilian wants is something like witness by proxy: Hazel's survival would not save Oscar or Jude, but it would prove that outlier children can be seen and accompanied, if not protected. Jeremy's speech — "we will defer to you… we won't force you" — is the closest the story comes to an ethics of care for children who cannot be normalised.
Lilian twice hedges her judgment of Ginny: "I did not think there was true malice in that laughter" — then seconds later: "Surely there was malice in that smile." This oscillation is deliberate. The story is not interested in rendering a verdict on Ginny but in exploring the limits of adult interpretation. The school's response is a case study in institutional failure: "male aggression" is taken seriously while Ginny's physical boundary violations go unnamed. Li seems to suggest that Ginny, too, is a child who doesn't fit, simply in a direction that society is more willing to accommodate.
Deferral & Structure
This is the story's most consequential structural choice, and readers often divide on it. The case for withholding: cause of death would convert the loss into a specific kind of tragedy (accident, illness, suicide, crime), each with its own cultural and emotional script. By refusing a cause, Li prevents the deaths from being categorised and thereby partially contained. We cannot say "ah, that kind of loss" and activate familiar patterns of consolation. The deaths remain radical and unassimilated — which is, arguably, closer to what grief feels like from inside. A good class discussion will test both positions against the story's stated hostility to "mandatory optimism."
The interruption is the story's most formally daring move. At the moment when the children are most beautiful, most silent, most alive, Li inserts a future scene in which the observer has lost everyone she is watching. "She wanted to exclaim, but the marvel was choked in her throat: neither Oscar nor Jude lived long enough to see a cloud of cicadas, rising, rising, rising." The triple repetition of "rising" is the only moment in the story that could be called lyrical excess — and it is immediately undercut. The digression enacts Lilian's condition: she cannot experience beauty without apprehending the loss that has outlasted it.
Endings & Meaning
Jeremy's speech proposes an alternative to both abandonment and coercion: patient accompaniment without a required outcome. He sets a time limit (13 hours, 10:30 the next morning) but makes no demands within it. He names Lilian as Hazel's companion. This is the story's most precise image of what parents can actually offer outlier children: presence, deferral of force, and an honest deadline. It does not save Hazel from her "outliers' lot"; the final paragraph makes clear that Lilian recognises in Hazel's eyes the same despair she will later see in Oscar's. But it offers a model of care that is not the same as rescue.
"Calm sea and prosperous voyage" is a wish — Goethe's lovers awaiting the wind that will bring reunion. Mendelssohn's setting moves from stillness to celebration: a dream of safe passage fulfilled. The Bakkhai line is its negative: "Farewell, my poor dear daughter. But you are beyond faring well!" — a father's last words to a child already beyond saving. The title holds both: the parental dream of a smooth crossing, and the tragic reality of hard faring. Li insists on the conjunction not as irony but as the full truth of parenthood: you carry both at once. The two translations of the Bakkhai line say "the same thing" according to Lilian. This is the story's final position: there is no correct version of the truth about losing a child.

Biography

Yiyun Li was born in Beijing in 1972 and trained as an immunologist before emigrating to the United States in 1996 to pursue graduate study at the University of Iowa. She abandoned science for fiction at Iowa, earning an MFA from the Writers' Workshop. She has written exclusively in English — her second language — since her early twenties, a choice she has described as a deliberate estrangement from her mother tongue and from a self she needed to leave behind.

Her debut collection, A Thousand Years of Good Prayers (2005), established her as a major voice in American fiction. Her memoir Dear Friend, from My Life I Write to You in Your Life (2017) chronicles two psychiatric hospitalisations and her relationship with literature as a form of survival. Her son Vincent Li died by suicide in 2019; much of her subsequent work, including Wednesday's Child and "Calm Sea and Hard Faring," engages — if never directly — with that loss.

She is a contributing writer at The New Yorker and teaches fiction at Princeton University.
